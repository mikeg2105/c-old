IMPORTANT CONCEPTS

In Flint terminology the complete geometry is made up of one or more connected four-sided regions . All regions will have four edges and four corners. A region can share any or all-four of its edges with other neighbouring regions. However, a single edge can only be shared by no more than two regions. In fact Flint geometry is always analogous to a fish-net where each hole in the net defines a region. The shape of these holes (regions) can be distorted by stretching, shrinking or distorting each of the four sides providing the logical connectivity of the fishnet topology is kept intact.

At first glance, this seems to impose strict constraints on the types of geometry which can be generated but in practice this is not the case and any intricate geometry can be generated by reducing the mesh size. As we shall see later, any disadvantages of having to build the geometry by four sided regions is far outweighed by the almost trivial way of in which grids can be generated for these geometries.
